Fishing Fundamentals: Knots & Baiting

Beginner-friendly tutorials for essential knots and safe baiting techniques. Includes a decision guide, common mistakes, and troubleshooting.

Tutorials About & Sources

Quick Start

If you’re brand new, use this workflow: pick the knot based on the job, then choose a bait rig based on water/cover. Retie after snags or big fish.

Choose a Knot (fast)

  • Hook/lure to mono/fluoro: Improved Clinch
  • Hook/lure to braid: Palomar
  • All-around option: Uni
  • More lure action: Non-Slip Loop
  • Join main line + leader: Double Surgeon’s

Safety & Reliability

  • Wet knots before tightening (reduces friction + weak spots).
  • Tighten slowly so coils seat neatly (don’t “snap” tight).
  • Trim tag ends; leave ~1/8 inch for slick braid/fluoro.
  • After a snag, check abrasion and retie if line feels rough.

Decision Guide

Use this table when you don’t know which knot to pick. The “why” helps you understand the tradeoffs.

If you need to… Use this knot Why it’s a good choice
Tie on a hook quickly with mono/fluoro Improved Clinch Simple, reliable, and easy to inspect.
Get strong performance with braid Palomar Doubled line creates a very strong, clean tie-in.
Use one knot for lots of situations Uni Versatile and works well on many line types.
Let your lure swing more naturally Non-Slip Loop Loop allows more movement than a fixed knot.
Connect leader to main line fast Double Surgeon’s Quick line-to-line connection with minimal fuss.

Knots

Each knot includes steps, when to use it, and what usually goes wrong.

1) Improved Clinch Knot

A classic for attaching hooks and small lures to mono/fluoro.

  1. Pass the line through the hook eye and pull 6–8 inches of tag end.
  2. Wrap tag end around the standing line 5–7 times (more wraps for thinner line).
  3. Feed the tag end through the small loop near the hook eye.
  4. Then feed the tag end through the larger loop you just created.
  5. Wet the knot, tighten slowly by pulling the standing line, then trim the tag.

When to use: Mono/fluoro for hooks and smaller lures.

Common mistakes: Tightening dry, wraps crossing over each other, too few wraps.

Pro tip: If the coils look messy, cut it and retie—messy coils often mean weaker knots.

2) Palomar Knot

One of the best beginner knots—strong and easy, especially on braid.

  1. Double 6–8 inches of line and pass the loop through the eye.
  2. Tie a loose overhand knot with the doubled line.
  3. Pass the hook/lure through the big loop.
  4. Wet and pull both the standing line and tag end to seat it cleanly.
  5. Trim the tag end.

When to use: Great on braid; also works well on mono/fluoro.

Common mistakes: Twisting the loop, cinching down before the loop is over the lure.

Pro tip: If your lure has a bulky body, make the loop extra large so it slips over easily.

3) Uni Knot

Versatile knot that works for hooks, swivels, and more.

  1. Run the line through the eye and double back to form a loop.
  2. Wrap the tag end around the doubled lines and through the loop 6–10 times.
  3. Wet the coils and snug them down evenly.
  4. Slide the knot to the eye by pulling the standing line.
  5. Trim the tag end.

When to use: All-around knot for many tackle connections.

Common mistakes: Uneven wraps, tightening too fast so coils stack poorly.

Pro tip: Pull the tag end first to form coils, then pull the standing line to slide to the eye.

4) Non-Slip Loop Knot

Creates a loop so your lure moves more naturally.

  1. Tie a loose overhand knot about 6 inches from the tag end.
  2. Pass the tag end through the hook eye, then back through the overhand knot.
  3. Wrap the tag end around the standing line 3–5 times.
  4. Pass the tag end back through the overhand knot.
  5. Wet, adjust loop size, tighten slowly, and trim.

When to use: Crankbaits, jigs, and lures where action matters.

Common mistakes: Loop too small, forgetting the final pass through the overhand knot.

Pro tip: If you want more action, make the loop slightly larger (but not huge).

5) Double Surgeon’s Knot

Quick way to join main line to a leader.

  1. Overlap main line and leader 6–8 inches.
  2. Form a loop using both lines.
  3. Pass both tag ends through the loop twice.
  4. Wet and pull all four ends evenly until snug.
  5. Trim tag ends.

When to use: Quick leader connection; great for beginners.

Common mistakes: Tightening one side only (causes uneven cinch).

Pro tip: If you have time and need a slimmer knot, you can use more advanced leader knots later.

Baiting Techniques

Three beginner-safe methods with notes on when each shines.

1) Live Worm (Threading)

Simple, effective, and great for panfish/bass.

  1. Hold the worm near one end to control movement.
  2. Insert the hook point and thread the worm up the shank.
  3. Reinsert once or twice so most of the hook is covered.
  4. Leave a short tail for movement in the water.

When to use: Calm water, light current, or when fish are finicky.

Common mistakes: Leaving too much hook exposed (short strikes) or too much worm dangling (steals).

Pro tip: If small fish keep stealing, shorten the tail and thread more of the worm.

2) Live Minnow (Lip vs. Back Hook)

Good for larger fish; hooking choice affects swim behavior.

  1. Lip hook: through the upper lip (or both lips) for casting/current.
  2. Back hook: just behind the dorsal fin (avoid the spine) for freer swimming.
  3. Keep handling gentle; don’t squeeze—minnows bruise easily.
  4. Check frequently and replace if the bait is weak or torn.

When to use: When you want a lively, natural presentation for bigger fish.

Common mistakes: Hooking too deep or hitting the spine (kills bait quickly).

Pro tip: In current, lip hooking helps minnows face forward and swim naturally.

3) Soft Plastic (Texas Rig)

A weedless rig that’s great around grass, brush, and cover.

  1. Insert hook point into the head, exit the belly, and slide bait up.
  2. Rotate the hook so it aligns with the body.
  3. Reinsert hook point into the body where it sits straight.
  4. Skin-hook the tip for weedless presentation.

When to use: Fishing weeds/brush, skipping under cover, or slow bottom presentations.

Common mistakes: Bait rigged crooked (spins), hook point exposed (snags).

Pro tip: If it spins on retrieve, re-rig until the body is perfectly straight.

Troubleshooting

My knot slips or breaks — what’s wrong?

Most failures come from tightening dry, crossed coils, or using a knot that doesn’t match the line type. Wet the knot, tighten slowly, and retie if the coils look uneven or stacked.

My line looks “fuzzy” after a snag.

That’s abrasion. Cut back past the damaged section and retie. Abraded line loses strength fast—especially on rocks and wood.

My soft plastic keeps sliding down the hook.

Rig it straighter, and slide the head fully up to the hook bend. If it still slips, consider a bait keeper style hook (optional upgrade).

My bait keeps getting stolen.

Thread more of the worm on the hook and shorten the tail. For minnows, use a smaller hook or switch to soft plastics if nuisance fish are heavy.
